## Overview

Most model-free reinforcement learning (RL) methods, despite their flexibility and empirical success, lack any formal guarantees of safety or convergence. This limitation becomes critical when deploying such agents in physical robotic systems, especially under stochastic dynamics or near safety-critical constraints. The CALF framework (Critic As Lyapunov Function) addresses this issue by introducing a constraint-based update mechanism that ensures goal-reaching guarantees throughout the learning process.

## Theoretical Basis

The core idea is to interpret the action-value function as a surrogate Lyapunov function and to constrain its evolution accordingly. This leads to a provable guarantee that, regardless of the critic's architecture or the specific optimization method employed, the policy sequence produced by CALF will preserve the goal-reaching property of the baseline. Theoretical analysis in the paper (Theorem 1) confirms that the agent converges to the target set with high probability under standard assumptions. Importantly, this holds even in the presence of stochastic transitions and without requiring any model of the environment.

## Experimental Validation

The proposed method was evaluated on a real TurtleBot3 Burger performing a navigation task in a planar environment. The scenario included a designated goal state and a penalized hazardous area in which the robot’s speed was restricted. The learning objective was to minimize cumulative cost while ensuring convergence to the goal.

CALF was compared against Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), a modified SARSA agent without recovery fallback, a nominal controller in polar coordinates, and a model predictive control (MPC) policy with access to full dynamics. All agents were given equal initialization, and the training duration was deliberately limited to a few episodes to highlight sample efficiency.

CALF consistently achieved a 100% success rate in reaching the goal, outperforming PPO and SARSA-m both in convergence reliability and in accumulated cost. Its performance approached that of MPC, despite requiring no model and being significantly lighter computationally. These results demonstrate that the transfer of knowledge from π₀, combined with constraint-aware learning, enables fast and reliable improvement within safety-preserving boundaries.

## Conclusion

CALF presents a principled and practical solution for reinforcement learning in safety-critical settings. It demonstrates that formal guarantees, typically associated with model-based methods or restricted control classes, can be retained even in fully model-free and data-driven frameworks. By embedding the structure of Lyapunov reasoning into critic training, the method opens new directions for stability-preserving RL applicable to real-world robotics.
